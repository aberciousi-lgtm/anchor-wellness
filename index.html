Designed around support, not surveillance

The HTML concept follows the design spec closely: warm earth tones, soft sage accents, rounded cards, compassionate copy, and a simple four-tab structure that stays usable during overwhelming moments.

📈

Mood Tracker

Users can log bipolar-specific mood states like depressed, low, baseline, elevated, and manic, with optional notes that feel reflective rather than clinical.

  • Large tap targets for easy check-ins
  • Optional substance-use note without shame
  • Observational streak and insight cards
🎤

Voice Check-in

A voluntary voice journal flow lets someone record how they feel, receive a transcription, and get a short, empathetic AI reflection plus gentle coping suggestions.

  • Text and voice modes in one journal space
  • Grounding prompts after reflection
  • Designed to feel private and supportive
⚗

Crisis Access

Crisis support stays visible without turning the whole interface into an alarm screen, using gentle rose accents and a one-tap path to 988 and trusted-contact outreach.

  • Quiet but persistent access point
  • Non-alarming visual treatment
  • Works alongside grounding tools

Screen concepts

The app structure stays intentionally compact: Home, Journal, Grounding, and Companion. That mirrors the spec's recommendation to keep choices simple and emotionally manageable.

Home tab

The landing screen prioritizes one-glance emotional support with a greeting, mood selector, optional notes, substance log, streaks, and an "I need help now" pathway.

DepressedLowBaselineElevatedManic

Journal tab

The journal combines text and voice check-ins, then returns a short reflection that sounds validating, not diagnostic, with small next-step prompts like breathing, water, or texting support.

I feel low today and I kind of want to shut everything off.
It sounds like today feels heavy and draining. You do not have to solve the whole day right now--want to try one small reset, like box breathing or texting someone safe?

Grounding that feels gentle

The Grounding tab uses more breathing room, fewer words, and subtle motion. The spec recommends visual calm over information density so the interface helps regulate, not overwhelm.

🤴

Box breathing

A soft animated orb expands and contracts to guide paced breathing without sharp timers or aggressive cues.

🌳

5-4-3-2-1 reset

The senses exercise breaks the moment into manageable steps that bring attention back to the body and environment.

5Name five things you can see.
4Notice four things you can feel.
3Listen for three sounds around you.

AI companion tone

The companion is designed to validate first, then offer one manageable next step. That reflects the spec's emphasis on empathetic, non-diagnostic support and quick-access coping prompts.

💬

Warm language

Microcopy avoids harsh clinical language and instead uses phrases like "Want to add a note?" and "You do not have to do this alone."

🧠

Supportive prompts

Suggestion chips like "Help me slow down," "I feel low today," and "Ground me" reduce friction when it is hard to type or think clearly.

🔒

Privacy-first structure

The design direction is based on voluntary check-ins and quiet support rather than passive monitoring, which better fits trust and emotional safety.
